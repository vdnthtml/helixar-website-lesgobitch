# Competitor Audit — Design Reference

---

## Tools We Learn From (Design Only)

These are not competitors. They are teachers.

---

## Linear

**URL:** linear.app
**What to steal:** Everything about information density. Linear proves you can have lots of data on screen without it feeling crowded. The key: consistent vertical rhythm, tight but not cramped spacing, text hierarchy that does the work.

**Specific elements to reference:**
- Sidebar nav: item height, icon size, active state
- Issue list: row height, status badges, metadata layout
- Keyboard-first feel (everything has a shortcut)
- Loading states (instant-feeling, skeleton screens)

**What NOT to copy:** Their product metaphors (issues, cycles, projects) — Helixar has different concepts.

---

## Raycast

**URL:** raycast.com (landing page)
**What to steal:** The hero section. Specifically how they use a dark background + subtle grid + one accent color to create depth without noise. Also the command palette interaction pattern.

**Specific elements:**
- Hero: Eyebrow → large headline → subhead → CTA pair → product screenshot
- The subtle background grid (very low opacity dot/line pattern)
- How they show the app in a "floating" screenshot with shadow below

**What NOT to copy:** Their specific gradient style — too associated with Raycast.

---

## Vercel / Resend

**URL:** vercel.com, resend.com
**What to steal:** Restraint. Vercel's landing page is sparse and it works because everything earns its place. Resend is a masterclass in "we don't need to prove ourselves" confidence.

**Specific elements:**
- Footer design (clean, organized, not overwhelming)
- Pricing section structure
- Feature comparison tables
- The "from the team that built X" social proof style

---

## Arc Browser

**URL:** arc.net
**What to steal:** How they sell an emotional shift, not a feature list. Arc doesn't say "tab management" — they say "the browser that puts you in control." Study how their copy works.

**Specific elements:**
- Narrative-first landing page structure
- How they use video/animation without being gimmicky
- Testimonial presentation (real, specific, from real people)

---

## Notion

**URL:** notion.so
**What to steal:** Empty state design. Notion's empty states are invitations, not voids. Also their onboarding flow — low friction, high momentum.

**Specific elements:**
- Empty state illustrations/icons (concept, not style)
- Onboarding step structure
- Template gallery concept (could apply to Helixar campaign templates)

---

## Dia

**URL:** dia.so (if available)
**What to steal:** The "AI-native workspace" visual language. How they show AI working alongside you rather than replacing you.

---

## Apple (macOS / App Design)

**What to steal:** Nothing flashy. The discipline of never having a UI element that doesn't earn its place. The way they handle:
- System fonts and text hierarchy
- Tab bars and navigation
- Progress indicators
- Confirmation dialogs

Apple's HIG (Human Interface Guidelines) is required reading for the Mac app.
URL: developer.apple.com/design/human-interface-guidelines

## What We Are NOT Building

| Reference | What they do | Why we don't copy it |
|---|---|---|
| HubSpot | Feature-heavy dashboard | Too corporate, too much blue |
| Hootsuite | Social scheduling grid | Wrong mental model entirely |
| Sprout Social | Analytics-first | Feels like a reporting tool, not a workspace |
| Jasper | AI content generation | Too generic, no intelligence layer |
| Buffer | Simple and approachable | Underpowered feeling, wrong positioning |
